US employment is at-will, and there is no federal statutory severance — so instead of a severance formula, these pages honestly explain what you're legally owed when a job ends. That means your accrued PTO/vacation (which some states require to be paid out), your final paycheck (with state deadlines), and your rights under the WARN Act to advance notice of a mass layoff.

These are sourced reference pages, not calculators — because the honest answer in most cases depends on your state and your employer's written policy. Always confirm with your state labor department.

Frequently asked questions

Is severance pay required by law in the US?

No. US employment is at-will and there is no federal law requiring severance pay. Severance is only owed if your employer's policy, an employment contract, or a WARN Act situation provides for it.

When must I receive my final paycheck?

It depends on your state. Some states (such as California) require immediate payment when an employer ends the job; others allow payment by the next regular payday. Check your state labor department — see our final paycheck by state guide.

Do I get paid for unused PTO when I leave a US job?

It varies by state. States like California, Illinois and Massachusetts treat accrued vacation as earned wages that must be paid out; other states permit 'use-it-or-lose-it' policies. See our PTO payout by state page for the details.

What is the WARN Act?

The federal WARN Act requires employers with 100+ employees to give 60 days' written notice of a mass layoff or plant closing. If they fail to, affected workers may be owed back pay and benefits for each day of missing notice, up to 60 days.
