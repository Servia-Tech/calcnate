When a Canadian job ends without cause, the law sets a statutory minimum you're owed — but it's often just a floor. The Ontario Employment Standards Act provides termination (notice) pay up to 8 weeks and, for long-service employees of larger employers, separate severance pay up to 26 weeks. Federally regulated jobs follow the Canada Labour Code instead, with graduated notice and its own severance formula.

You're also owed accrued vacation pay (4%/6% in Ontario, 4%/6%/8% federally). Estimate each below — and remember common-law reasonable notice can exceed these minimums.

Frequently asked questions

How is termination pay calculated in Ontario?

Under the Employment Standards Act, notice or pay in lieu is 1 week after 3 months, 2 weeks after 1 year, then 1 week per completed year up to a maximum of 8 weeks. Separate statutory severance pay (1 week per year, up to 26 weeks) applies only if you have 5+ years of service and the employer's payroll is $2.5M or more, or 50+ employees are let go.

Is ESA severance the same as common-law severance?

No. The ESA figures are the legal minimum. Employees without an enforceable termination clause are often entitled to much larger common-law reasonable notice (frequently several months). For that, speak to an employment lawyer.

How much vacation pay am I owed in Canada?

In Ontario, vacation pay is 4% of gross wages, rising to 6% after 5 years. Federally regulated employees get 4% (1–5 years), 6% (5–10 years) and 8% (10+ years). Any accrued unpaid vacation pay must be paid out on termination.

Does federally regulated termination pay differ?

Yes. Under the Canada Labour Code graduated scale (from Feb 1 2024), notice or pay is 2 weeks from 3 months, 3 weeks at 3 years, then +1 week per year up to 8 weeks, plus severance of the greater of 2 days' pay per year or 5 days' pay.
